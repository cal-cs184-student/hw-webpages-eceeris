Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection (20 Points)

Walk through the ray generation and primitive intersection parts of the rendering pipeline.

To transform the image coordinates (x, y) from normalized space to world space, I mapped them to the camera sensor plane at z=-1. Using field of view (hFov, vFov), I calculated camera space coordinates: x_cam = (x - 0.5) × 2 × tan(hFov_rad / 2), y_cam = (y - 0.5) × 2 × tan(vFov_rad / 2), and z_cam = -1. Then I transformed this direction vector to world space using the rotation matrix: V_world = (c2w × V_cam).unit(). This creates a ray from camera's world position in the direction V_world, between near and far planes. In my loop to convert pixels to rays, I implemented supersampling by generating multiple rays via randomized offsets within each pixel. By averaging the radiance returned from each of these rays, I antialiased the image, resulting in smoother edges. To implement supersampling, I created a loop that generates ns_aa samples for each pixel. For each sample, I used get_sample() to achieve the random 2D offset within the boundaries, then I added the offset to the pixel's integer coordinates (x,y) and normalized by image dimensions. This produced a random sample point that I passed to generate_ray() to create the world-space ray, then I averaged the radiance results to determine the final color.


Explain the triangle intersection algorithm you implemented in your own words.

For the triangle primitives, I implemented the Moller-Trumbore Algorithm because it solves for the intersection of rays and triangles directly with barycentric coordinates without needing to use the plane equation. I first calculated the edge vectors E1 = P2 - P1 and E2 = P3 - P1. Then I used the determinant of the system to check if the ray is parallel to the triangle (when determinant is approx zero, no intersection occurs). If a valid t is found within the ray’s interval [min_t, max_t], I check if the coordinates u, v, and (1-u-v) are all nonnegative summing to <=1. If they are, I update max_t to the current t and interpolate the vertex normals with barycentric weights (1 - u - v, u, v) to ensure smooth shading across the surface. For sphere primitives, I implemented the ray-sphere intersection test by solving the quadratic equation derived from substituting the ray equation into the algebraic representation of a sphere. I calculated the coefficients a, b, and c using the ray's origin and direction relative to the sphere's center and radius (a = D ⋅ D, b = 2(O - C) ⋅ D, and c = (O - C) ⋅ (O - C) - R²). By checking the discriminant (b² - 4ac), I determined if the ray intersects the sphere. If the discriminant is non-negative, I solved for the two potential intersection times (t₁ and t₂) and selected the nearest t within the ray’s valid range [min_t, max_t]. For confirmed intersections, I again updated the ray's max_t and computed the surface normal at the hit point as the unit vector pointing from the sphere's center to the intersection point (n̂ = (P - C) / R).

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy (20 Points)

Walk through your BVH construction algorithm. Explain the heuristic you chose for picking the splitting point.

For my BVH construction algorithm, I computed the bounding box of all primitives in the current node by expanding an empty bounding box, then I checked base case against max_leaf_size for my recursive function. Then for the recursive part, I check if node is interior node, I split along the axis according to my heuristic using std: :partition to divide into left and right collections and I force the split to be evenly split in half to prevent infinite recursion. Finally I recursively call the construction function for the left and right children. For the splitting heuristic, I chose the axis with the widest dimension of bounding box, and the split point was the average of the centroids of all the bounding boxes along that axis. I chose it because the longest axis ensures the volume is reduced as efficiently as possible.

Part 3: Direct Illumination (20 Points)

Walk through both implementations of the direct lighting function.

Uniform Hemisphere Sampling: To estimate direct lighting using this method, I uniformly sampled directions in a hemisphere centered around the hit point using a constant PDF 1/2pi. For each sampled direction, I cast a shadow ray into the scene. If the ray intersects a light source (checked via the emission of the intersected BSDF), I calculate its contribution using the reflection equation: multiplying the incoming emission by the surface's BSDF and the cosine of the incoming angle, and finally averaging across all samples. While unbiased, this method is highly inefficient because many rays are cast into empty space, resulting in significant noise. Importance (Light) Sampling: Instead of shooting rays blindly, importance sampling loops directly over all light sources in the scene. For area lights, I take N samples; for point lights, we only take 1 sample to save computational time. I calculated the vector pointing from the hit point directly to the sampled point on the light. I then cast a shadow ray in that specific direction. If the BVH confirms there are no intersections before reaching the light's distance (meaning the light isn't blocked), I accumulated the radiance. Because we are guaranteeing our rays point toward light sources, this method drastically reduces variance and produces much smoother renders for the same computational effort.

Sampling with more light rays per area decreases the variance and noise within the soft shadows dramatically. At only 1 and 4 light rays, the Monte Carlo estimator can't accurately calculate partial occlusions, which results in scattered high-contrast pixels. At 16 and 64 light rays, the estimator has enough samples to more accurately average the blocked and unblocked rays, and the result is much more smooth with softer shadows and gradients.


Compare the results between uniform hemisphere sampling and lighting sampling in a one-paragraph analysis.

Uniform hemisphere sampling produces dramatically more variance and noise compared to importance sampling for the same number of rays. With uniform hemisphere sampling, the Monte Carlo estimator casts rays randomly in all directions, meaning a large percentage of the rays completely miss the light sources and return zero radiance. As a result, it produced a very grainy render filled with scattered, high-contrast pixels. In contrast, importance sampling casts shadow rays toward known light sources. Because this method guarantees that unblocked rays will contribute to the lighting calculation, the estimator calculates the illumination much more efficiently, and the result is a smoother render with softer shadows and cleaner gradients.


Part 4: Global Illumination (20 Points)

Walk through your implementation of the indirect lighting function.

I implemented the indirect lighting function to recursively simulate light bouncing multiple times through the screen. First I calculated the hit point and got the incoming ray direction in object space. If isAccumBounces is true, the function adds direct illumination (one bounce) to the total accumulated radiance, and the recursion stops at the base case r.depth <= 1. Then sample_f() samples a new direction w_in and its pdf. New rays are generated from the hit point (offset) with depth - 1, which calls the recursive function again if the new ray intersects the scene. I used a russian roulette with a continuation probability of 70% to prevent infinite recursion.
