Overview

I built a cloth simulator from a mass-spring system and integrated advanced GPU shaders, using Verlet integration for movement and spatial hashing to handle self-collision complexity. I also used Blinn-Phong, texture mapping, displacement mapping, and mirror reflections for the lighting models. Overall, this project broke down complex, realistic behaviors into simple local constraints.

Part 1: Masses and springs

I implemented buildGrid by iterating through a nested loop to create point masses: For HORIZONTAL orientation, I set the y-coordinate to 1.0, and for VERTICAL, I varied y while adding a small random z-offset (between \(-0.001\) and \(0.001\)) to prevent instability during collision. After populating the masses, I established structural, shearing, and bending springs by checking the neighbors of each point mass to ensure all internal forces were correctly linked.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Impact of Spring Constant (\(k_s\))

At a low \(k_s\) like \(500\text{ N/m}\), the cloth is thinner, with less support, and is sinking more heavily with gravity than it does with a higher \(k_s\) like \(50,000\text{ N/m}\). This is because \(k_s\) scales directly with \(F\), as \(F = k_s(L - L_{rest})\), so a greater spring constant means the restorative force keeping the springs within the mesh at the original length is greater.

Impact of Density

At a low density (\(5\text{ g/cm}^2\)), the cloth appears lighter, while at high density (\(150\text{ g/cm}^2\)) the sag in the cloth is more pronounced. This is because \(F_g = mg\), and increasing density increases the mass \(m\), so there is more downwards force on the springs.

Impact of Damping

At \(0\%\) damping, the cloth vibrates and glitches because it is falling forever (no energy loss). At \(10\%\) damping, the cloth falls slowly then stops promptly at the bottom. At \(90\%\) damping the cloth falls quickly but with extreme resistance to swinging. This is because damping represents a loss of energy, and the damping factor \(d\) scales the velocity according to the Verlet integration step: \[x_{t+dt} = x_t + (1 - d)(x_t - x_{t-dt}) + a \cdot dt^2\]

A lower spring constant allows the cloth to more closely hug the sphere with many folds, while a higher spring constant keeps the cloth's shape more rigid with less folds, resists fully falling around the sphere. This is because a higher spring constant implies a stronger restorative force pushing against the stretching.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Self-Collision Lifecycle

In this section, I observed the cloth falling and folding onto itself using scene/selfCollision.json. My implementation of spatial hashing prevents the cloth from intersecting itself by having point masses repel each other.

Impact of Density

Higher density increases the gravitational force \(F_g = mg\), which forces the cloth to compress into a tighter, more intricate pile with significantly more overlapping layers compared to the lower density version.

Impact of Spring Constant (\(k_s\))

As \(k_s\) increases the restorative force \(F = k_s(L - L_{rest})\) also increases. This makes the cloth behave like a thicker, stiffer material that resists tight folds. However, low \(k_s\) results in a more compliant mesh that collapses into a dense, flat pile.

Technical Implementation Details

To achieve efficient self-collision detection, I implemented spatial hashing, reducing the complexity from \(O(N^2)\) to \(O(N)\).

This self-collision algorithm prevents the cloth from intersecting itself by ensuring a minimum distance of \(2 \cdot \text{thickness}\) between any two point masses. To avoid the \(O(N^2)\) brute-force check, I implemented the hash_position function to map a 3D coordinate to a unique floating-point hash representing a specific grid volume. I defined the box dimensions as: \[w = 3 \cdot \frac{\text{width}}{\text{num_width_points}}\] \[h = 3 \cdot \frac{\text{height}}{\text{num_height_points}}\] \[t = \max(w, h)\] Using these dimensions, I convert the point mass's \((x, y, z)\) coordinates to their respective 3D grid indices using the floor function. These indices are then combined into a single hash value using large prime numbers to minimize collisions: hash = (gx * 104729) ^ (gy * 7919) ^ (gz * 619). Additionally, at each simulation step, build_spatial_map clears the existing hash map and adds back all the point masses. Then, for each point mass, self_collide looks up the masses within its grid volume. If any neighboring point mass (other than itself) is within a distance of \(2 \cdot \text{thickness}\), a correction vector pushes the point mass away until the distance is exactly \(2 \cdot \text{thickness}\). These corrections are averaged and applied to the point's current position, scaled by \(1/\text{simulation_steps}\) for stability.

Part 5: Shaders

What is a shader program?

A shader program is code runs on the GPU to handle the rendering pipeline, consisting of vertex shaders and fragment shaders. Vertex shaders process individual vertices, transforming 3D positions into their 2D screen coordinates. They also pass along data like normals and UV coordinates to the fragment shader. The fragment shader processes every pixel, taking the data from the vertex shaders to calculate final colors based on the textures, lighting models, and material properties. Together, they create complex visual effects by simulating how light interacts with surfaces at a parallelized scale.

Blinn-Phong Shading Model

The Blinn-Phong model approximates the appearance of surfaces by combining three distinct lighting components: ambient, diffuse, and specular. Ambient light is the consistent base light added everywhere, diffuse light is a matte reflection that depends on the angle between the light and the surface normal (Lambertian reflection: \(k_d (I/r^2) \max(0, n \cdot l)\)). Specular light appears near the reflection of the light source: it uses "half-vector" between the view and light directions (\(k_s (I/r^2) \max(0, n \cdot h)^p\)).

Texture Mapping

In this section, the fragment shader samples a custom texture using the interpolated UV coordinates from the mesh.

Bump vs. Displacement Mapping

Bump Mapping: Modifies the surface normals based on a height map to simulate detail. The geometry remains smooth, which is visible on the silhouette of the sphere.

Displacement Mapping: Also physically displaces the vertex positions in the vertex shader. This results in a jagged, more realistic silhouette where the geometry is altered.

The comparison shows that while Bump Mapping looks consistent across resolutions, Displacement Mapping is dependent on mesh density. At 16x16, the displacement is blocky and doesn't capture grid detail. At 128x128, the physical vertices are dense enough to accurately represent the height map, resulting in a smooth and jagged silhouette.

Coarseness Comparison: At a low resolution (-o 16 -a 16), displacement mapping looks blocky because there aren't enough vertices to represent the height map. At high resolution (-o 128 -a 128), the surface looks significantly more refined and matches the texture perfectly.

Mirror Shader

I calculated the reflection vector \(\mathbf{r}\) to implement the mirror shader: Given the vector from the surface to the camera \(\mathbf{w_o}\) and the surface normal \(\mathbf{n}\), the reflection vector is: \[\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{w_i} - 2(\mathbf{w_i} \cdot \mathbf{n})\mathbf{n}\] where \(\mathbf{w_i} = -\mathbf{w_o}\). I then sampled u_texture_cubemap using this direction \(\mathbf{r}\) to find the color of the reflected environment.
